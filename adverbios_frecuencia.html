📝 What are Adverbs of Frequency?

An adverb is a word that adds extra information about a verb — it usually tells us how, when, or how often something happens. Adverbs of frequency answer the question "How often...?" They go on a scale from 100% (it always happens) to 0% (it never happens).

100%alwaysI always drink coffee in the morning.
~90%usuallyShe usually walks her dog after school.
~70%oftenHe often plays the guitar at night.
~50%sometimesThey sometimes cook dinner together.
~10%hardly ever / rarely / seldomShe hardly ever loses her keys.
0%neverWe never miss the school bus.

📐 Word Order

POSITIVE SUBJECT→ ADVERB→ VERB She often reads books.
WITH "BE" SUBJECT→ BE→ ADVERB You are always funny. (also "there is/are": There is usually a lot of traffic.)
QUESTION AUXILIARY→ SUBJECT→ ADVERB Does she often read? / Are you always funny?
MODAL SUBJECT→ MODAL→ ADVERB→ VERB You should always wear a helmet.
MODAL Q. MODAL→ SUBJECT→ ADVERB→ VERB Should I always do it? / Can you usually come early?

🧩 Modal verbs (can, could, should, must, will, might...) work like "be": the adverb goes right after the modal and before the main verb — Subject + Modal + Adverb + Verb. In questions, it's the same order as with "be" but the modal moves to the front — Modal + Subject + Adverb + Verb: "Should I always do it?" / "Can you usually come early?"

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Position with a verb❌ She goes usually to work.✅ She usually goes to work.
Position with "be"❌ They usually are tired.✅ They are usually tired.
Negative sentences❌ We often don't go out.✅ We don't often go out.
Never / Hardly ever❌ He isn't never late.✅ He is never late.
Modal verbs❌ You always should wear a helmet.✅ You should always wear a helmet.

⏱️ Frequency expressions like once a day, twice a week, three times a month, every day are longer (2+ words) and go at the end of the sentence: "I brush my teeth three times a day." / "I see her every day."
